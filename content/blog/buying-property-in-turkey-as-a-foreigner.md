## Introduction

Turkey has become one of the most attractive real estate markets for international investors. With its strategic location, competitive property prices, and the Turkish Citizenship by Investment program, thousands of foreigners purchase property in Turkey every year. But navigating the legal landscape requires careful planning and professional guidance.

This guide covers the essential legal aspects of buying property in Turkey as a foreign national.

## Who Can Buy Property in Turkey?

Under Turkish law, citizens of most countries can purchase real estate in Turkey. The principle of reciprocity was largely abolished in 2012, opening the market to buyers from over 180 countries. However, citizens of certain countries — including Syria, North Korea, and Cuba — face restrictions.

**Key restrictions to be aware of:**

- Foreign nationals cannot purchase property in military zones or security areas
- There is a maximum land ownership limit of 30 hectares per foreign individual
- Total foreign ownership in a district cannot exceed 10% of the district's total area

## The Buying Process Step by Step

### 1. Finding the Property

Work with a licensed real estate agent and ensure the property has a clean title deed (*tapu*). It is advisable to hire an independent lawyer to conduct due diligence before making any commitment.

### 2. Due Diligence

Your lawyer should verify:

- The title deed is legitimate and free of encumbrances
- There are no outstanding debts, mortgages, or liens on the property
- The property complies with local zoning regulations
- Building permits and occupancy certificates are in order

### 3. Obtaining a Tax Number

Every foreign buyer must obtain a Turkish tax identification number (*vergi numarası*) from the local tax office. This is a straightforward process that requires only your passport.

### 4. Opening a Turkish Bank Account

You will need a Turkish bank account to complete the transaction. Most banks require your passport, tax number, and proof of address.

### 5. Title Deed Transfer

The official transfer takes place at the Land Registry Office (*Tapu Müdürlüğü*). Both parties — or their authorized representatives — must be present. A sworn translator is required if the buyer does not speak Turkish.

## Costs and Taxes

When purchasing property in Turkey, budget for the following:

- **Title deed transfer tax:** 4% of the declared property value (typically split between buyer and seller)
- **Annual property tax:** 0.1%–0.6% depending on property type and location
- **Earthquake insurance (DASK):** Mandatory for all residential properties
- **Legal fees:** Typically 1%–2% of the property value
- **Agent commission:** Usually 2%–3%

## Turkish Citizenship by Investment

Foreign buyers who purchase property valued at $400,000 or more may be eligible for Turkish citizenship. The property must be held for at least three years, and the purchase must be made from a Turkish citizen or a Turkish company.
